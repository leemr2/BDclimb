## What "critical force" means

Below a certain intensity, your forearms can keep working almost indefinitely — energy supply keeps pace with demand. Above it, fatigue compounds quickly and failure is only a matter of time. That tipping point is your critical force. Training right at it teaches your forearms to hold a higher sustainable output, which is exactly what a long route asks for.

## How CFB differs from IHE

Intermittent hang endurance at 60% builds rhythmic, repeatable endurance — many sub-maximal reps. Critical-force blocks push closer to the threshold itself: fewer reps, higher intensity, with the goal of holding form and force stable late in each block. IHE widens the tank; CFB raises the ceiling. Alternating them across Mesocycle 2 prevents accommodation and trains the system from both directions.

## Getting the intensity right

CFB lives at RPE 8–9: hard, but something you could sustain with stable force, not an all-out effort. The calibration check after each block matters. If you select "too hard," the safety monitor will prompt you to drop the load 5–10% for the remaining blocks. Too easy and you are not near the threshold; inconsistent and your rests or load need adjusting.

## Why we compare across cycles

A single CFB session is noisy. The objective signal is the trend: comparing the same protocol across Mesocycle 2 shows whether your threshold is genuinely rising. Stable or increasing force at the same load, block to block and week to week, is the cleanest evidence that the threshold work is doing its job.
