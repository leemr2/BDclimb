## Where you are now

The aerobic base you built in Mesocycle 1 raised the ceiling on how much work your forearms can sustain before pump becomes limiting. Mesocycle 2 spends that capacity: it adds harder, more anaerobic work and sharpens the crux-after-fatigue skill against a more specific target.

## The two threshold tools

This phase trains the threshold between sustainable and non-sustainable forearm output from two angles:

- **Intermittent hang endurance (IHE)** at 60% of your max hang builds rhythmic endurance — the ability to repeat sub-maximal contractions many times without force dropping off.
- **Critical-force blocks (CFB)** train the intensity ceiling itself — the point above which fatigue accumulates non-linearly. They run at RPE 8–9, sustainable but not maximal.

They alternate rather than replacing one another because they target the same energy system differently, and alternating prevents your body from accommodating to either one.

## Crux simulation gets specific

Your crux-after-fatigue work now uses a crux that resembles your goal route. The lead-in fatigues you; the crux tests whether you can still execute. Remember the progression rule: only one variable changes per week, and only when the trigger is met. Success rate above 60% earns a longer lead-in first; round count and crux difficulty come later.

## Anaerobic capacity work

The 4×4 boulder sets and route-style intervals develop repeatability under incomplete rest — the exact quality a sustained route demands. Keep problems 2–4 grades below your limit on 4×4s so the stimulus stays metabolic rather than maximal. Watch your weekly sRPE: this is the highest-load phase, and the safety monitor will flag spikes over the 20–25% guideline.
