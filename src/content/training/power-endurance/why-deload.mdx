## The PE injury-risk equation

In some programs a deload is a strong suggestion. In power-endurance it is mandatory, and the reason is arithmetic. PE training generates high finger loading (max hangs, intermittent endurance, critical-force), high metabolic stress (4×4s, intervals, crux simulation), and high neuromuscular demand (power and pulling work) — often in the same week. The cumulative fatigue is higher than any single modality produces on its own, and it accumulates faster than it announces itself.

## What your sRPE data says

Look at your weekly load for the past three weeks. That accumulation is by design — it is the stimulus. But stimulus is not adaptation. Adaptation happens when the load drops and your body consolidates what the hard weeks demanded. The deload reduces volume sharply while keeping enough intensity to hold your edge.

## The shoulder trap

Shoulder symptom scores that have been quietly creeping up over a mesocycle often normalize during the deload. Shoulder overuse is the second most common climbing injury after finger pulley strains, and PE programs load it heavily through pulling and dynamic movement. If your score has been rising, watch it this week — a deload is the cheapest insurance you have.

## Retest as data, not a chore

Your Week 4 intermittent endurance and crux-after-fatigue retests are not "just tests." They set your Mesocycle 2 working loads and tell you whether the aerobic base from the last three weeks is actually transferring to the crux. Test with the same protocols you used at Week 0 so the comparison means something.
