## Why baseline testing matters

Power-endurance training stacks three demanding stimuli — high finger loads, high metabolic stress, and hard movement under fatigue. Starting any of them at the wrong level either wastes the block or builds an overtraining debt you spend weeks repaying. The Week 0 assessment exists to set the right floor for each, calibrated to your body on test day rather than to a theoretical average climber.

## What Week 0 measures

Your assessment establishes four numbers:

- **Finger max strength** (7-second max hang, 20–22 mm edge, half-crimp) sets the intensity for every hangboard session and the 60% working load for intermittent hang endurance.
- **Intermittent endurance** (repeaters at 60% of max until failure) tells us how much interval volume your forearms can actually absorb at the start of the program.
- **Crux-after-fatigue simulation** establishes your real baseline on the most specific skill in the program: executing a hard sequence when you are already pumped.
- **Injury baseline**, including a shoulder symptom score, gives us a reference point for the safety monitor that runs after every session.

## Why crux-after-fatigue is the headline

You can probably do the crux moves fresh. The performance question is whether you can do them three minutes into a route with your forearms 70% pumped. The crux-after-fatigue test measures exactly that, and every subsequent progression in the crux work is built from your real baseline score — not an estimate.

## What to expect

Testing takes one focused session. Warm up thoroughly, take full rest between attempts, keep grip positions consistent, and log honestly — including failures. These same protocols repeat at Weeks 4, 8, and 12, so consistency now makes every retest comparison meaningful.
