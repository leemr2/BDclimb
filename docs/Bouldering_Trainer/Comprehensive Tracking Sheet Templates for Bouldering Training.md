# Comprehensive Tracking Sheet Templates for Bouldering Training

These templates work for 2-day, 3-day, and 4-day bouldering training plans. They're designed to be simple enough to maintain consistently while capturing the essential data the research identifies as important for progression and injury prevention in bouldering.

------

## How to Use These Templates

### For 2-Day Plan:

- Use Templates 1 (baseline), 2 (weekly), 3A/3C (as relevant), 4 (testing), 5 (monthly)
- **Template 3C (Limit Bouldering) is CRITICAL** - track every limit session
- Focus on Template 2 weekly summary—critical for managing limited frequency
- Use daily quick-log (Template 6) on rest days to track recovery

### For 3-Day Plan:

- Same as 2-day, all templates apply
- Weekly session log (Template 2) becomes primary tool
- Detailed workout logs (3A-3G) optional but recommended for key sessions
- **Template 3C for every limit session**
- **Template 3A for every max hang session**

### For 4-Day Plan:

- **ALL templates highly recommended** due to higher volume
- Template 2 (weekly log) is essential for managing 4-session load
- Template 3 detailed logs help optimize each session type
- Template 5 (monthly dashboard) critical for catching overtraining trends
- **Template 3A, 3B, 3C tracking every relevant session is non-negotiable**

### Minimum Viable Tracking (if time-constrained):

1. **Template 1**: Baseline assessment (Week 0 only)
2. **Template 2**: Weekly session log (every week—NON-NEGOTIABLE)
3. **Template 3C**: Limit bouldering log (EVERY limit session)
4. **Template 4**: Progress testing (Weeks 4, 8, 12)
5. **Template 6**: Daily quick-log for recovery monitoring

**Research basis:** The training load monitoring literature shows that session-RPE is a valid, practical internal load measure. The injury prevention research emphasizes load management and symptom tracking as modifiable risk factors. For bouldering, max hang performance and limit boulder efficiency are your most direct measures of goal achievement—track them religiously.

## Special Notes for Bouldering Training

### Why Max Hang and Limit Boulder Tracking is Critical

Bouldering performance depends heavily on:

- **Maximal finger force** (max hangs track this)
- **Power/RFD** (campus and dynamic bouldering track this)
- **Movement efficiency** (attempts-to-send tracks this)

**This means:**

- Template 3A (Max Hangs) and 3C (Limit Bouldering) are not optional
- Max hang load progression is your primary strength KPI
- Attempts-to-send on benchmarks shows efficiency improvements
- You need to track these metrics across every relevant session

### Load Management is Even More Critical

Bouldering training combines:

- High finger loads (max hangs, small holds)
- High neural demand (explosive moves, coordination)
- High impact forces (dynamic moves, falls)
- High skill complexity (technique under load)

**This creates higher injury risk than lower-intensity climbing.**

**Therefore:**

- Weekly sRPE monitoring is mandatory (Template 2)
- Week-to-week increases >15-20% are dangerous for bouldering
- Deload weeks (4 & 8) are injury prevention, not optional
- Finger pain >3/10 = immediate session modification

### Balancing Intensity Across Sessions

For 4-day plans especially, you must balance:

- **2 max strength days** (Monday/Thursday: max hangs)
- **1 power day** (Monday in Meso 2: campus work)
- **1-2 recovery days** (Tuesday: antagonists/technique)
- **2-3 limit boulder days** (spread across week)

**If you turn recovery days into hard sessions**, you will accumulate fatigue faster than you can recover, leading to:

- Declining max hang performance
- Injury risk (especially fingers)
- Motivation drop
- Overtraining

**Use Template 2 weekly summary to catch this early.**

### Campus Board Safety

Campus training is **optional** and **high-risk**:

- Only use in Mesocycle 2 (Weeks 5-7)
- Skip entirely if any finger/shoulder pain >1/10
- Track every campus session with Template 3B
- Stop immediately if form degrades
