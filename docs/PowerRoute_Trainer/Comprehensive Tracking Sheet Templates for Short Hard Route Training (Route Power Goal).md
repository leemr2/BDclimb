# Comprehensive Tracking Sheet Templates for Short Hard Route Training (Route Power Goal)

These templates work for 2-day, 3-day, and 4-day training plans. They're designed to be simple enough to maintain consistently while capturing the essential data the research identifies as important for progression and injury prevention in power-endurance climbing on short, hard routes.

## Special Notes for Route Power Training

### Why PE Interval Tracking is Critical

Unlike pure strength training where you track max hangs, or endurance training where you track volume, **route power training requires tracking your ability to sustain high-intensity climbing with incomplete recovery**. This is what intervals measure.

**This means:**

- Template 3C (PE Intervals) is not optional during Mesocycle 2
- Total rounds completed at target intensity is your primary KPI
- Pump progression across rounds tells you if you're pacing correctly
- If interval capacity isn't improving over 4-6 weeks, something needs to change

### Why Strength Maintenance Matters

Power-endurance without sufficient max strength = poor performance ceiling. You need:

- Template 3A (Max hangs) tracked consistently in Weeks 1-4
- Maintenance tracking in Weeks 5-12 to ensure no regression
- The goal is NOT to keep improving max strength forever, but to keep it high enough that route cruxes are submaximal

### Load Management is Critical

Short hard route training combines:

- High finger loads (max hangs)
- High metabolic stress (PE intervals)
- High neural demand (power work, campus)
- Route-specific practice (variable intensity)

**This creates significant injury risk if not managed carefully.**

**Therefore:**

- Weekly sRPE monitoring is mandatory (Template 2)
- Week-to-week increases >20% are dangerous
- Deload weeks (4 & 8) are injury prevention, not optional
- Finger pain >3/10 = immediate session modification
- Back-to-back high-intensity days (for 3-4 day plans) require excellent recovery

### Balancing Multiple Stimuli

For 3-4 day plans especially, you must balance:

- **Max strength days** (high CNS demand, low volume)
- **PE interval days** (high metabolic demand, moderate CNS)
- **ARC/recovery days** (must stay easy - this is not optional)
- **Route practice days** (variable, skill-focused)

**If you turn recovery days into hard sessions**, you will:

- Accumulate fatigue faster than recovery
- See declining PE interval performance
- Increase injury risk dramatically
- Lose motivation

**Use Template 2 weekly summary to catch this early.**

### Performance Metrics Hierarchy

**Primary KPI (most important):**

1. PE interval work capacity (rounds completed at target intensity)
2. Benchmark route time/pump rating

**Secondary KPIs (supporting):**

1. Max hang load (maintain in M2-M3, build in M1)
2. Intermittent endurance reps
3. Project route progress

**Tertiary KPIs (nice to have):**

1. Power metrics (campus, limit boulders)
2. Pull-up strength
3. Movement quality ratings

Focus your tracking energy on #1-2. If those improve, you're succeeding at route power development.

1. **Primary KPI shifted**: PE intervals > crux-after-fatigue drills

3. **Endurance de-emphasized**: Less ARC volume, more intensity focus

### What Stayed the Same:

- Weekly symptom tracking
- Deload structure and timing
- Progressive testing at Weeks 4, 8, 12
- Injury prevention protocols

### Why These Changes:

Short hard routes fail from:

- Insufficient power/contact strength (not max finger strength alone)
- Inability to sustain near-maximal output (not aerobic capacity)
- Poor recovery between hard sequences (not long-duration pacing)

The tracking must reflect these different limiting factors.
