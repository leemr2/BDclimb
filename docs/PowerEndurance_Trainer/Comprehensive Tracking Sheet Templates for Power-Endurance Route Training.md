# Comprehensive Tracking Sheet Templates for Power-Endurance Route Training 

These templates work for 2-day, 3-day, and 4-day training plans. They're designed to be simple enough to maintain consistently while capturing the essential data the research identifies as important for progression and injury prevention in power-endurance climbing with late cruxes.

- **Template 3E (Crux-After-Fatigue) is CRITICAL** - this is your most important session
- Focus on Template 2 weekly summary—critical for managing limited frequency
- Use daily quick-log (Template 6) on rest days to track recovery

### For 3-Day Plan:

- Same as 2-day, all templates apply
- Weekly session log (Template 2) becomes primary tool
- Detailed workout logs (3A-3E) optional but recommended for key sessions
- **Template 3E is your primary performance indicator**

### For 4-Day Plan:

- **ALL templates highly recommended** due to higher volume
- Template 2 (weekly log) is essential for managing 4-session load
- Template 3 detailed logs help optimize each session type
- Template 5 (monthly dashboard) critical for catching overtraining trends
- **Template 3E tracking every week is non-negotiable**

### Minimum Viable Tracking (if time-constrained):

1. **Template 1**: Baseline assessment (Week 0 only)
2. **Template 2**: Weekly session log (every week—NON-NEGOTIABLE)
3. **Template 3E**: Crux-after-fatigue log (EVERY session—this is your goal!)
4. **Template 4**: Progress testing (Weeks 4, 8, 12)
5. **Template 6**: Daily quick-log for recovery monitoring

**Research basis:** The training load monitoring literature shows that session-RPE is a valid, practical internal load measure. The injury prevention research emphasizes load management and symptom tracking as modifiable risk factors. For power-endurance with late cruxes, the crux-after-fatigue performance metric is your most direct measure of goal achievement—track it religiously.

## Special Notes for Power-Endurance Training

### Why Crux-After-Fatigue Tracking is Critical

Unlike endurance training where you're primarily tracking volume and pump management, power-endurance training with late cruxes requires tracking a **specific performance outcome**: can you execute hard moves when pumped?

**This means:**

- Template 3E (Crux-After-Fatigue) is not optional
- Success rate is your primary KPI, not just volume or intensity
- You need to track this metric across every session to see progression
- If success rate isn't improving over 4-6 weeks, something needs to change

### Load Management is Even More Critical

Power-endurance training combines:

- High finger loads (strength work)
- High metabolic stress (intervals, PE work)
- High neural demand (crux execution)
- High volume (multiple sessions per week)

**This creates higher injury risk than pure endurance or pure strength training.**

**Therefore:**

- Weekly sRPE monitoring is mandatory (Template 2)
- Week-to-week increases >20-25% are dangerous
- Deload weeks (4 & 8) are injury prevention, not optional
- Finger pain >3/10 = immediate session modification

### Balancing Volume Across Sessions

For 4-day plans especially, you must balance:

- **1 high-intensity strength day** (max hangs, campus)
- **1 aerobic/recovery day** (ARC, must stay easy)
- **1 high-intensity endurance day** (intervals, intermittent hangs)
- **1 specific practice day** (crux-after-fatigue, routes)

**If you turn the aerobic day into another hard session**, you will accumulate fatigue faster than you can recover, leading to:

- Declining crux-after-fatigue performance
- Injury risk
- Motivation drop
- Overtraining

**Use Template 2 weekly summary to catch this early.**
