# Comprehensive Tracking Sheet Templates for Power-Endurance Route Training

These templates work for 2-day, 3-day, and 4-day training plans. They're designed to be simple enough to maintain consistently while capturing the essential data the research identifies as important for progression and injury prevention in power-endurance climbing with late cruxes.

------

## Special Notes for Power-Endurance Training

### Why Template 3E is Non-Negotiable

Success rate on the crux-after-fatigue drill is your primary KPI. If it isn't tracked every session, you cannot tell whether the training is working or whether to adjust programming. All other templates serve this one metric.

### Why Template 3G (4×4) Matters

The 4×4 fills a distinct gap — raw anaerobic capacity under accumulating fatigue — that the crux simulation doesn't fully cover. Track falls-per-round and late-round quality: these reveal whether the capacity is actually building. If round count climbs but late-round falls stay constant, the problem selection may be too easy.

### Why Template 3H (Critical-Force Blocks) is a Benchmark Session

Critical-force blocks anchor the intensity boundary between sustainable and non-sustainable output. Use the cross-cycle comparison table in 3H every time you run this session — it's your most objective measure of threshold adaptation across the mesocycle.

### Why Silent Feet and Fluency Counts Belong in Template 3B

The ARC session is low intensity by design, which means counting slips and stops costs nothing in extra fatigue or time. These numbers give you a movement economy trend line across the full 12 weeks. If stop count stays flat while everything else improves, your limiting factor may be tactical (pacing/reading) rather than physiological.

### Why the Antagonist Circuit Must Be Tracked

The shoulder-stability circuit is the primary structural injury-prevention measure in all three plans. Shoulder symptoms should go down or stay flat across the 12 weeks. If the symptom score in Template 3A or the monthly trend in Template 5 shows it rising, the circuit should be bumped to 3 rounds and the campus volume should be reviewed.

### Load Management is Even More Critical for PE

Power-endurance training combines high finger loads, high metabolic stress, high neural demand, and (in the 4-day plan) high weekly volume simultaneously. This creates higher injury risk than pure endurance or pure strength blocks. Weekly sRPE monitoring via Template 2 and the monthly load table in Template 5 are your early warning system. Week-to-week increases >20-25% are dangerous regardless of how you feel in the session.
