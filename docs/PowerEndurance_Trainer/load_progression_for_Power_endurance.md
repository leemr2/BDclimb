# Evidence-Based Load Progression for Power-Endurance Climbing Plans

## TL;DR
- The current rules (e.g., "+2-3kg after 2 perfect sessions"; "add volume → reduce rest → add difficulty") are **directionally sound and align with mainstream exercise-physiology principles**, but they are simplistic heuristics, not validated protocols — the strongest single upgrade is to replace fixed time-based triggers with **autoregulated, readiness-based progression** (RPE/repetitions-in-reserve plus a margin-to-failure rule), because autoregulated methods outperform fixed percentage-based progression for strength in meta-analysis.
- For fingers, the binding constraint is **connective-tissue lag**: muscle and neural adaptation occur within weeks, but tendons/pulleys remodel over months-to-years, so finger-load progression should be the **slowest, most conservatively capped** variable in the whole plan — small percentage steps (~2-5%), confirmed by performance and symptom checks, not a fixed kilogram increment.
- For power-endurance intervals, the literature supports progressing **volume/density before intensity**, anchoring endurance intensity to **critical force (~41% MVC) rather than %MVC alone**, monitoring weekly load with **session-RPE**, and treating the "10% rule" as a soft guide rather than a hard law (it is not well supported as a strict threshold; the acute:chronic workload framing is conceptually better but also contested).

## Key Findings

1. **Progressive overload is universal, but the *model* matters less than consistent overload plus autoregulation.** ACSM's position stands define progressive overload as "the gradual increase in stress placed upon the body" and recommend manipulating load, volume, rest, frequency and exercise selection. Meta-analyses find periodized training beats non-periodized, and daily undulating periodization (DUP) tends to edge out linear periodization for strength in trained lifters — but several high-quality meta-analyses find no significant difference, so the effect is real but modest and contested.

2. **Supercompensation and block periodization give a clean theoretical scaffold for the 3-mesocycle structure.** Issurin's accumulation → transmutation → realization sequence maps directly onto a 12-week climbing plan: build capacity (volume, base finger strength), convert to event-specific power-endurance, then peak/taper. Issurin grounds these blocks explicitly in homeostatic regulation, stress adaptation and the law of supercompensation.

3. **Finger strength adapts fast; connective tissue does not.** Hangboard max-hang studies show ~10-28% finger-strength gains in 4-10 weeks, but tendon/pulley remodeling (thicker A2/A4 pulleys, flexor tendons) takes years of loading. Keith Baar's work indicates tendon collagen-synthesis signaling plateaus after ~10 minutes of loading and that connective tissue is slow to rebuild. This dual-adaptation mismatch is the central injury-risk driver in climbing finger training.

4. **Max-hang intensity is well-characterized; 80-95% MVC / ~7-10s holds with a margin to failure is the evidence-based zone.** Eva López-Rivera's protocols (using the "effort margin" concept from González-Badillo) deliberately stop short of failure, which research supports as reducing fatigue without sacrificing strength gains.

5. **Critical force is the best-validated endurance anchor — but its *trainability* is essentially unproven.** Giles et al. (2019, *Int J Sports Physiol Perform* 14(7):972-979) found climbers' mean critical force was 425.7 ± 82.8 N — i.e. **41.0 ± 6.2% MVC** — with W′ of 30,882 ± 11,820 N·s; the 2021 all-out test paper found CF as % body mass explained **61% of sport-climbing and 26% of bouldering performance**, with a combined CF+W′ model explaining **66% of sport and 44% of bouldering ability**. However, no published study yet demonstrates a statistically significant training-induced *increase* in CF; the one crossover study that measured CF pre/post found it was maintained, not raised.

6. **Session-RPE is a validated load-monitoring tool; the 10% rule is a weak heuristic.** sRPE (Foster's RPE × duration) is well validated across sports. The 10% weekly-increase rule has limited and conflicting support; the acute:chronic workload ratio (ACWR) refined it but has itself been heavily criticized and the original injury-risk figure was challenged with a formal retraction request.

## Details

### 1. Core progressive-overload models

**ACSM guidance.** The ACSM 2009 position stand "Progression Models in Resistance Training for Healthy Adults" defines progression as "the act of moving forward or advancing towards a specific goal over time" and progressive overload as "the gradual increase in stress placed upon the body during exercise training." For strength it recommends progressively increasing intensity; for local muscular endurance it recommends light-to-moderate loads (40-60% 1RM), high repetitions (>15) and short rest (<90s). The 2025-era update (an "Overview of Reviews" summarizing 137+ studies and 30,000+ participants) reaffirms progressive overload as essential and uses a "smallest detectable effect size" of ~3.96% to define a minimum effective dose for strength.

**Linear vs undulating.** The evidence is genuinely mixed. Some analyses favor undulation (the Prestes et al. 2009 study, *J Strength Cond Res* 23(9):2437-2442, compared LP vs DUP in 40 men with >1yr training over 12 weeks; both groups improved significantly in bench, leg-press and arm-curl 1RM, with DUP numerically larger — though several reviews note the between-group difference was not always statistically robust, so the specific magnitudes should be cited cautiously). Crucially, the **Harries, Lubans & Callister (2015) meta-analysis found no significant differences between linear and undulating models**, and a 2017 PeerJ meta-analysis (DOI 10.7717/peerj.3695) put hypertrophy change at 3.9% ± 3.0% (LP) vs 5.1% ± 4.2% (DUP) — a small, non-decisive edge. Net read: undulation helps trained athletes modestly and is worth building into climbing weeks (a max-hang day and a power-endurance day inherently differ in intensity/volume), but it is not magic.

**Block periodization.** Issurin defines blocks as 2-4 week mesocycles of "highly concentrated workloads": accumulation (basic abilities — aerobic base, strength), transmutation (sport-specific anaerobic/power-endurance), realization (peaking/taper). His *Sports Med* 2019 review ties these explicitly to supercompensation. Residual training effects mean the aerobic/strength base built in accumulation persists to support the higher-intensity transmutation block.

**Minimum effective dose / maximum adaptive stimulus.** The minimum-effective-dose literature shows even ~1-3 sets, 1-3×/week at >80% 1RM produces meaningful 1RM gains (a meta-analysis estimated ~12kg overall 1RM increase from the lowest-dose groups). Volume drives hypertrophy more than strength, with a dose-response up to an individual ceiling. Practical implication for time-limited climbers: a modest, well-progressed finger-training dose is sufficient; chasing maximum volume mainly raises injury and fatigue cost.

**Supercompensation.** Yakovlev's classic four-phase model (load → fatigue → recovery → supercompensation) implies load increases should be timed to land in the supercompensation window. Practically, energy systems recover in hours-to-days; neural/muscular adaptation across 48-72h; connective tissue far slower — so the "right" inter-session interval differs by tissue and training quality.

### 2. Finger/grip (hangboard) load progression

**How to quantify isometric load.** Two equivalent levers: **Maximum Added Weight (MAW)** on a fixed edge (e.g., 18-20mm) and **Minimum Edge Depth (MED)** at bodyweight. López-Rivera's research tested 4 weeks MAW (18mm) then 4 weeks MED. The max-hang intensity zone is 80-95% MVC (effectively a load you could hold ~3s longer than the prescribed ~7-10s hang — the "effort margin").

**Rate of adaptation and connective-tissue lag.** Hangboard interventions show fast contractile/neural gains: Hermans, Saeterbakken, Vereide, Nord, Stien & Andersen (*Front Sports Act Living* 2022, 4:888158) ran an RCT of 35 intermediate-to-advanced climbers (8 women, 27 men) using two 48-min Beastmaker 1000 sessions/week for 10 weeks; the hangboard group significantly out-gained controls in 23mm-rung peak force and average force (p = 0.001-0.031, ES = 0.29-0.66), with within-group gains of roughly +17% peak force, +18% average force, +28% RFD and +12% dead-hang endurance (controls unchanged). Notably, the endurance and RFD gains were *not* significantly superior to climbing alone. Their progression came from moving up the app's difficulty levels (5A→7C) rather than added weight, with the first two weeks deliberately held at low resistance to adapt tissue. By contrast, structural connective-tissue adaptation (Schweizer; Schreiber/Schöffl 10-year ultrasound follow-up) shows pulley and tendon thickening accrues over *years*. Kjær's collagen-synthesis review states tendon adaptation time to chronic loading is far longer than for muscle, and that 12-14 weeks of resistance training raising strength up to ~21% need not change tendon cross-sectional area. This is the physiological basis for capping finger-load progression rate.

**Is the "+2-3kg after 2 perfect sessions" rule supported?** There is **no direct study validating this specific rule.** It is a reasonable double-progression heuristic, but it has two weaknesses: (a) a fixed kilogram step is a *larger relative jump for weaker climbers* (2.5kg on a 30kg-added climber is ~8%; on a 70kg-added elite it's ~3.5%), so it should be expressed as a **percentage**; (b) "2 perfect sessions" is a reasonable readiness gate but should incorporate a margin-to-failure / RPE check rather than a binary "perfect."

**Percentage vs RPE/feel.** Evidence favors autoregulation. Per the 2025 network meta-analysis from Shanghai University of Sport (PubMed 40791980), back-squat 1RM SUCRA rankings were **APRE (93.0%), then RPE (66.8%), VBRT (27.0%), and percentage-based training (13.2%)** — confirming "APRE, VBRT, and RPE were significantly more effective than PBRT in enhancing maximum strength." For fingers specifically, where day-to-day skin, tendon and pump status vary, an RPE/margin-based gate is likely safer and at least as effective as fixed percentages.

### 3. Power-endurance interval load progression

**Quantifying and progressing.** Classic climbing power-endurance protocols: bouldering 4×4 (four problems × four sets, ~4-min rests, problems ~2-3 grades below max), 30/30 intervals, and intermittent hangboard repeaters (7:3 work:rest). Load is progressed via problem difficulty, number of reps/sets, work:rest ratio, and total work done.

**Volume before intensity.** The existing rule ("add reps/duration → add sets → reduce rest → increase difficulty") is consistent with ACSM-style progression and with the principle that intensity (the most fatiguing, highest-injury-risk variable) should be progressed last. Rest-interval reduction is a legitimate density/load lever: shortening rest from 3min→1min reliably cuts reps-per-set (e.g., bench ~27→21 reps) and raises metabolic/perceptual stress, but for *strength* preservation, longer rests are superior — so rest reduction suits the power-endurance/metabolic blocks, not the max-strength blocks.

**Critical force training.** Lattice Training practitioners (Tom Randall, Ollie Torr) anchor power-endurance repeaters to %MVC: ~60% MVC at 7:3 to failure for power-endurance, ~40% MVC for aerobic endurance, with a 50-70% band for strength/power-endurance and 30-45% for endurance. The critical-power/critical-force model implies that to raise the sustainable force ceiling you should train **at or just below CF and progress by total work done**, while W′ (the finite reserve above CF) is trained with severe-domain intervals above CF. Critically, Baláš et al.'s validation study (*Eur J Appl Physiol* 2024, DOI 10.1007/s00421-024-05490-7) warns that "CF, based on the mean force of the last three contractions, failed to reliably predict the highest sustainable work rate. In contrast, determining CF as the end-force of the last three contractions exhibited a stronger link to sustainable work" — when load was lowered by 2/4/6 kg, 38%/69%/92% of climbers could complete a 720s task. So anchor training slightly *below* the computed 4-min-test CF.

**Session-RPE and weekly load.** sRPE = RPE (Borg CR-10) × session minutes, giving arbitrary units; sum across the week for weekly load, and compute monotony/strain (Foster). This is well validated across sports and athlete age groups. For week-over-week progression, the "10% rule" is popular but weakly evidenced: **Buist et al. (2008, *Am J Sports Med* 36(1):33-39), the GRONORUN RCT of novice runners, found "no effect of a graded training program (13 weeks)... applying the 10% rule, on the incidence of running-related injury compared with a standard 8-week training program"** — injuries were ~20-21% in both groups despite the graded group increasing weekly load only 10.5% vs the standard group's 23.7%. The ACWR (acute 7-day ÷ chronic 28-day load; "sweet spot" 0.8-1.3) is a better conceptual tool but has been formally criticized (Impellizzeri and colleagues), and the original injury-risk figure was the subject of a retraction request. Use sRPE trends and the 0.8-1.3 ACWR band as guardrails, not gospel.

### 4. Periodization across mesocycles

**Mapping to the 3-mesocycle structure.** Accumulation = base finger strength + aerobic capacity + technique volume; transmutation = power-endurance intervals, CF/W′ work, crux-after-fatigue; realization = taper + performance. This is a direct, defensible application of Issurin.

**Deloads.** Survey/Delphi evidence: strength/physique athletes deload on average every ~5.6 weeks for ~6.4 days, primarily cutting volume (reps and sets) while keeping frequency, sometimes dropping intensity ~10%. A 1-week deload mid-program did not impair gains in controlled trials. For climbing, with its connective-tissue risk, a planned deload every ~3-4 weeks (or readiness-triggered) with ~40-60% volume reduction is prudent; some experts argue deloading every few weeks is too frequent for non-fatigued athletes, which supports a readiness trigger.

**Readiness- vs time-based progression.** This is the highest-leverage change. Autoregulation, ACWR and sRPE all point toward triggering progression and deloads on athlete-readiness signals (performance, RPE, soreness, sleep, morning symptoms) rather than a fixed calendar. Gabbett explicitly notes the 10% figure is only a "guide" and that load tolerance and chronic-load context govern how much to add.

### 5. Climbing-specific considerations

- **Dual adaptation:** Because finger muscle/neural drive outpaces tendon/pulley remodeling, the finger-load progression rate must be the rate-limiter for the whole plan. Dave MacLeod's widely cited view (*9 Out of 10 Climbers Make the Same Mistakes*) is that most climbers overemphasize finger strength relative to technique/endurance, and that finger training must be informed by total climbing volume and weaknesses.
- **Critical force transferred from cycling:** CF is the climbing analog of critical power; W′ the analog of anaerobic work capacity. Training prescription mirrors cycling: raise CF with threshold/just-below-CF work progressed by volume; develop W′ with above-CF intervals.
- **Climbing-specific progression evidence is thin.** Most hangboard intervention studies are 4-10 weeks, small samples, often no control group or no strength measure, and varied edge sizes — so effect sizes transfer cautiously. López-Rivera & González-Badillo (2019, *J Hum Kinet* 66:183-195) compared three 8-week programs in 26 advanced climbers (7c+/8a) and found intermittent dead-hangs (repeaters) raised grip endurance +25.2% at 4 weeks and +45% at 8 weeks, while max-hangs raised it +34.1% at 8 weeks — but with no control group and an atypical 11mm test edge.

### 6. Practical formalization (proposed evidence-based rules)

**a) Max-hang intensity (replacing "+2-3kg after 2 perfect sessions"):**
- Work at 85-92% MVC, ~7-10s holds with a 2-3s margin to failure (López-Rivera effort margin).
- Express progression as **% added load, not fixed kg**: add ~2-5% only when the climber completes the prescribed sets at target RPE ≤8 (comfortable margin / ≥2 RIR-equivalent) for 2 consecutive sessions AND reports no finger symptoms.
- Cap finger-load increases at roughly one step per microcycle; if RPE rises or symptoms appear, hold or regress.

**b) Interval volume/intensity (replacing the informal "one variable at a time"):**
- Keep the ordered hierarchy but formalize gates: progress **reps/duration → sets → rest reduction → difficulty**, advancing a variable only when the current prescription is completed at target sRPE and technique stays intact (technique breakdown = session over).
- Quantify rest reductions meaningfully (e.g., 10-20% steps, ~30s on a 4-min rest), and cap weekly load growth using sRPE trend within roughly an ACWR of 1.0-1.3.

**c) Critical-force block training:**
- Test CF/MVC (4-min all-out or repeater-to-failure), anchor repeaters ~5-10% *below* the computed CF (Baláš caution), progress primarily by **total work done** (sets/duration) before raising %MVC; periodically retest MVC and CF (~every 8-12 weeks).

**d) Crux-after-fatigue simulation complexity:**
- Progress complexity, not just load: increase crux difficulty, lengthen the pre-fatigue section, or shorten recovery before the crux — one variable per step, gated on completion at target RPE. This trains W′ depletion-then-redline, mirroring real routes.

## Recommendations
1. **Switch the master progression trigger from time-based to readiness-based.** Adopt an RPE/RIR + margin-to-failure gate plus simple wellness checks (sleep, soreness, finger symptoms). This is the single best-supported upgrade (APRE/RPE > percentage-based for strength). **Threshold to hold/regress:** if RPE for the prescribed load climbs ≥1 point session-to-session, or any finger pain appears, hold or back off.
2. **Re-express the finger-load step as a percentage (~2-5%), not fixed kg,** and make finger-load the slowest-progressing variable in the plan. Keep the "2 sessions" confirmation but add the RPE/symptom gate.
3. **Adopt session-RPE load monitoring** (RPE × minutes; weekly sums; monotony/strain). Use ACWR 0.8-1.3 as a soft guardrail, not the 10% rule as a hard law.
4. **Anchor endurance intensity to critical force (~41% MVC as a starting reference; test individually),** progress CF blocks by total work done, and train W′ with above-CF intervals. Retest every 8-12 weeks.
5. **Plan deloads at ~3-4 week intervals OR on readiness triggers, cutting volume ~40-60%** while holding frequency; retain a readiness-triggered option.
6. **Preserve the volume-before-intensity hierarchy for intervals** but formalize the gates and quantify rest steps; use rest reduction in metabolic/power-endurance blocks, not max-strength blocks.
7. **For the 2/3/4-day variants:** apply DUP-style intensity variation across the week, ensure ≥48h between high-intensity finger sessions for connective-tissue recovery, and scale total weekly finger-load volume to the chronic load the athlete has demonstrated.

## Caveats
- **The trainability of critical force is unproven.** CF is an excellent test/anchor but no published study shows a significant training-induced CF increase; treat CF-targeted progression as theory-informed, not evidence-proven.
- **The "+2-3kg / 2 perfect sessions" and "one variable at a time" rules have no direct validation studies**; they are reasonable heuristics consistent with general principles, which is why the recommendations refine rather than discard them.
- **Climbing hangboard intervention studies are small, short, and methodologically varied** (no control groups, differing edge sizes, surrogate endurance measures), so the percentage gains cited transfer with uncertainty.
- **Periodization-model superiority is modest and contested** — consistent overload + autoregulation + adequate recovery matter more than the specific model label. The Prestes et al. magnitude figures in particular should be verified against the primary text before publication, as reviews disagree on whether the LP–DUP difference reached significance.
- **Load-monitoring tools (sRPE, ACWR, 10% rule) are decision aids, not predictors**; the ACWR injury-risk model has been formally challenged.
- Several practitioner sources (Lattice podcasts and coaching articles, Hörst and MacLeod books) are credible and widely used but are **not peer-reviewed experimental evidence**, and are flagged as such where used.
